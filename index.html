Economic Story

Main result of this paper are two theorems stating general conditions under which a competitive equilibrium exists

  • If every individual has initially some positive quantity of every commodity available for sale, then a competitive equilibrium exists.
  • Competitive equilibrium exists if there are some types of labour with the following properties:
    • Each individual can supply some positive amount of at least one such type of labor;
    • Each such type of labor has a positive usefulness in the production of desired commodities.

A3 Consumer Preferences

$\forall x'_i$, the sets $\{x_i | x_i \in X_i , x_i \succsim x'_i\}$ and $\{x_i | x_i \in X_i , x'_i \succsim x_i\}$ are closed in $X_i$

C1 Profit maximization

$y_j^*$ maximizes $p^*\cdot y_j$ over the set $Y_j$ for all $j$

Where $p^* \cdot y_j$ (profit) is the inner product i.e,

\[\begin{align*}p^*\cdot y_j &= \sum^l_{h=1}p^{*}_{h}\cdot y_{jh} \\ &= p^*_1y_{j1} + p^*_2y_{j2} + ... + p^*_ly_{jl}\end{align*}\]

Given an equilibrium price vector $p^*$, firm $j$ is maximizing profit for each $j$

C2 Distribution of Wealth and Income

$x^*_i$ maximizes $u_i(x_i)$ over the set $\{x_i | x_i \in X_i, p^*\cdot x_i \leqq p^*\cdot \zeta_i + \sum_{j=1}^n\alpha_{ij}(p^*\cdot y^*_j)\}$

Objective is to maximize utility among all consumption vectors while satisfying the budget restraint

...the cost of the utility maximizing consumption vector should not exceed individual income

$p^*\cdot x_i$ represents the expenditure on consumption bundle $x_i$

$p^*\cdot \zeta_i$ represents the revenue from selling endowment $\zeta_i$

$\sum_{j=1}^n\alpha_{ij}(p^*\cdot y^*_j)$ denotes the share of profit earned from production plan $y^*_j$

C3 Pricing Systems and Equilibrium

$p^* \in P = \{p | p \in \R^l, p \geqq 0, \sum_{h=1}^l p = 1\}$

$p$ non-negative and $p$ normalized so that price of commodities sum to 1

Nature of equilibrium

Supply and demand of all commodities are treated as functions of $p$

As $p$ varies to $p'$, producers and consumers treat $p'$ as the new equilibrium price vector

Market equilibrium occurs when supply for a commodity equals demand

Let $x = \sum_{i=1}^{m}x_i$, $y=\sum_{j=1}^{n}y_j$ and $\zeta = \sum_{i=1}^{m}\zeta_i$

Then $z = x - y - \zeta$ where $z$ is the excess of demand over supply

C4 Market Clearing

$z^* \leqq 0$, $p^* \cdot z^* = 0$

Demand exceeds supply $\implies$ price rises

Supply exceeds demand $\implies$ price falls

$z^* \leqq 0$ ensures that demand does not exceed supply in equilibrium i.e, prices do not rise indefinitely

However prices cannot be driven down indefinitely, recall $p^* \geqq 0$

Thus $p^*\cdot z^* = \sum_h p^*_hz^*_h = 0$ iff $p^*_h = 0$ and $z^*_h < 0$ (free good) OR $z^*_h = 0$ (demand meets supply) for all $h$ commodities

Double Take on Local Non-satitation

Recall non-satiation, there is some $x'_i$ such that $u_i(x'_i) > u_i(x_i)$. Is this true at $x^*_i$?

Assume it is true. Then by the convexity of $u_i$, it follows $u_i(x'_i) > u_i(x^*_i)$

Then there exists a profit maximizing $x'_i$ which also satisfies
$p^*\cdot x'_i \leqq p^*\cdot \zeta_i + \sum_{j=1}^n\alpha_{ij}(p^*\cdot y^*_j)$

Contradiction! At equilibrium, our original condition must be updated to reflect
$p^*\cdot x^*_i = p^*\cdot \zeta_i + \sum_{j=1}^n\alpha_{ij}(p^*\cdot y^*_j)$

That is, consumer $i$ should receive ALL of their income and cannot withold any of it at if $p^*_h>0$ at the most optimal consumption plan

That is, $z_h^* = x_h^* - y^*_h - \zeta_h = 0$ for all $h$

Competitive Equilibrium

A set of vectors $(x^*_1,...,x^*_m, y^*_1,...,y^*_n, p^*)$ is a competitive equilibrium if it satisfies the following conditions:

$y^*_j \in \argmax p^*\cdot y_j$ where $y_j \in Y^j$ and $\forall j$

$x^*_i \in \argmax u_i(x_i)$ where $x_i \in X_i$ and $p^*\cdot x_i \leqq p^*\cdot \zeta_i + \sum_{j=1}^n\alpha_{ij}(p^*\cdot y^*_j)$

$p^* \cdot z^* = 0$ where $z^*= x^* - y^* - \zeta^* \leqq 0$ and $p^* \geqq 0$

A Lemma on Abstract Economies

Let $U_\iota \subseteq \R^l$ for $\iota = (1....\upsilon)$

Let $U = U_1 \times U_2 \times ... \times U_\iota$

This represents the set of ordered $\upsilon$-tuples $a = (a_1,...,a_\upsilon)$ where $a_\iota \in U_\iota$

For each $\iota$, let $f_\iota: U \rightarrow \R$

Let $\overline{U_\iota} = U_1 \times U_2 \times ... \times U_{\iota-1} \times U_{\iota + 1} \times ... \times U_{\upsilon}$

This represents the set of ordered $(\upsilon -1)$-tuples $\overline{a_\iota} = (a_1,...,a_{\iota-1},a_{\iota+1},...,a_\upsilon)$ where $a'_\iota \in U'_\iota$ for all $\iota' \not = \iota$

Let $A_\iota(\overline{a}_\iota) \subseteq U_\iota$ be a function for each $\overline{a_\iota} \in \overline{U_\iota}$

Thus an abstract economy is defined as $[U_1,...,U_\upsilon,f_1,...,f_\upsilon,A_1(\overline{a_1}),...,A_\upsilon(\overline{a_\upsilon})]$

A Lemma on Abstract Economies

Since $A_\iota(\overline{a}_\iota) \subseteq U_\iota$ (where $A_\iota(a_\iota)$ is a continuous multi-valued function)

Let an agent $\iota$ choose an action $a_\iota \in U_\iota$

If $f_\iota(a)$ assigns an amount (payoff) received by agent $\iota$

Thus an abstract economy can treated closely to a game! Extending Nash definition of an equilibrium for a game...

$a^*$ is an equilibrium point of $[U_1,...,U_\upsilon,f_1,...,f_\upsilon,A_1(\overline{a_1}),...,A_\upsilon(\overline{a_\upsilon})]$ if for all $\iota = 1,..,\upsilon$, $a^*_\iota \in A_\iota(\overline{a}_\iota)$ and $f_\iota(a^*, \overline{a^*}) = \argmax f_\iota(a, \overline{a^*})$

An equilibrium is defined when each agent is maximizing their payoff over the set of their available actions given other agents' actions

A Lemma on Abstract Economies

If

For each $\iota$, $U_\iota$ (choice set) is compact and convex and $f_\iota(\overline{a_\iota}, a_\iota)$ (payoff function) is continuous on $U$ and quasi-concave in $a_\iota$

For every $a_\iota$, $A_\iota(\overline{a_\iota})$ (best response correspondence) is convex, nonempty and is a continuous function whose graph is a closed set

Then

The abstract economy $[U_1,...,U_\upsilon,f_1,...,f_\upsilon,A_1(\overline{a_1}),...,A_\upsilon(\overline{a_\upsilon})]$ has an equilibrium point

This lemma generalizes Nash's theorem on the existence of equilibrium points for games

Then define the abstract economy $E = [X_1,...,X_m,Y_1,...,Y_n,P, u_1(x_1),...,u_m(x_m),p\cdot y_1,...,p\cdot y_n, p\cdot z, A_1(\overline{x_1}),...,A_m(\overline{x_m})]$

There are $n$ firms, each chooses $y_j$ from $Y_j$ and receives payoff $p \cdot y_j$

Imaginary participant chooses $p \in P$ and receives $p \cdot z$

$max$ function is chosen to avoid $A_i(\overline{x_i})$ from being an empty set

Define $\hat{X_i}$, $\hat{Y_j}$

$\hat{X_i} = \{x_i | x_i \in X_i,$ there exists $x_{i'} \in X_{i'}$ for each $i' \neq i$, $y_j \in Y_j$ for each $j$ such that $z\leqq 0\}$

Compactness of $\hat{Y_j}$

For the sake of contradiction suppose $\hat{Y_1}$ is unbounded. Let $y^k_j \in Y_j$ and $x^k_i \in X_i$ be sequences

Let $\lim_{k \to \infty} |y^k_1| = \infty$ and $\sum_{j=1}^n y_j^k \geqq \sum_{i=1}^m x_i^k - \zeta$ (1)

Since $\xi = \sum_{i=1}^m \xi_i$ and $x_i \geqq \xi_i$ for all $i$, it follows $\sum_{j=1}^n y_j^k \geqq \xi - \zeta$ (2)

Let $\mu^k = max_j |y_j^k|$ such that $\mu^k \geqq 1$ for a large $k$.
Then by convexity of $Y_j: (1/\mu^k)y^k_j + (1-1/\mu^k)0 \implies y^k_j / \mu^k \in Y_j$(3)

Then from (2) $\sum_{j=1}^n(y_j^k/\mu^k) \geqq (\xi - \zeta)/\mu^k$(4)

Then $\lim_{k \to \infty} \mu^k = \infty$ from (1) and thus $|y_j^k / \mu^k| \leqq 1$(5)

By Bolzano-Weierstrass choose a subsequence $\{k_q\}$ such that for all $j$, $\lim_{q \to \infty} y^{k_q}_j/\mu^{k_q} = y_j^o$(6)

From (4) and (6) $\sum_{j=1}^n y_j^o \geqq 0$ and since $Y_j$ is closed, $y_j^o \in Y_j$(7)

Compactness of $\hat{Y_j}$

Since $\sum_{j=1}^n y_j^o \geqq 0$ and $y_j^o \in Y_j$ (by closure) then $\sum_{j=1}^n y_j^o \in Y$ (total production set)(8)

However since $\sum_{j=1}^n y_j^o \geqq 0$, then $\sum_{j=1}^n y_j^o = 0$ by A1 (at least one negative component needed)(9)

Rearranging (9) $\sum_{j\neq j'}y_j^o = -y^o_{j'}$(10)

Since $0 \in Y$ then $y_{j'}^o \in Y$ and thus $-y_{j'}^o \in -Y$ (additive inverse set)(11)

But from (10) $\sum_{j\neq {j'}}y_j^o = -y_{j'}^o \in Y$. So $-y_{j'}^o \in -Y$ and $-y_{j'}^o \in Y$ thus $y_{j'}^o = 0$ by A1(12)

Since $\lim_{q \to \infty} y^{k_q}_j/\mu^{k_q} = y_j^o = 0$, the equality $|y_j^{k_q}| = \mu^{k_q}$ is only true for a finite number of $q$ (13)

Considering the definition of $\mu^{k_q} = max_j|y^{k_q}|$, there exists a max L-2 norm for at least one $j$ for every $q$. Contradiction!(14)

Hence WLOG $\hat{Y_1}$ is bounded so is $\hat{Y_j}$ for all $j$. Since $\hat{Y_j}$ closed and bounded, it is compact.

Compactness of $\hat{X_i}$

Let $x_i \in \hat{X_i}$ then $\xi_i \leqq x_i \leqq \sum_{j=1}^{n} y_j - \sum_{i' \neq i} x_{i'} + \zeta$ (1)

Since $y_j \in \hat{Y_j}$ for all $j$ and $\xi_{i'} \leqq x_{i'}$ for all $i'$ then $\xi_i \leqq x_i \leqq \sum_{j=1}^{n} y_j - \sum_{i' \neq i} xi_{i'} + \zeta$ (2)

Since $\hat{Y_j}$ is bounded, the term $\sum_{j=1}^{n} y_j - \sum_{i' \neq i} xi_{i'} + \zeta$ is bounded (3)

Hence from (3), $\hat{X_i}$ is bounded for all $i$. It has been proven $\hat{X_i}$ and $\hat{Y_j}$ are compact.

This contradicts $y^*$ as an equilibrium for $\tilde{E}$

Thus $y^*_i$ maximizes $p^*\cdot y_j$ for $y_j \in Y_j$ (Condition 1)

Validate $p^*$ For Competitive Economy

Finally $p^*$ maximizes $p\cdot z^*$ for $p \in P$ is true for both $E$ and $\tilde{E}$ (Condition 3)

All conditions (C1-C4) for competitive equilibrium have been satisfied for $E$ under the assumptions (A1-A4), thus it is a competitive equilibrium at $(x^*_1,...,x^*_m,y^*_1,...,y^*_n,p^*)$
